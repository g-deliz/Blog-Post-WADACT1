Returning from a Knee Injury

Home Post 1 Post 2 Post 3

I've had a ball at my feet since I was three years old. My mom likes to tell people I was kicking things around the living room in Guaynabo before I could even talk in full sentences. So, when I tore something in my knee last year (right in the middle of pushing myself harder than ever here in Madrid) it wasn't just a physical injury. It felt like the floor disappeared from under the one thing that has defined my entire life.

I'm not going to pretend it was a small moment. There were nights I lay in my apartment, ice on my knee, FaceTiming my parents back home, wondering if this was it. Nineteen years old, thousands of miles from home, and suddenly unable to do the one thing I came to Spain to do.

The Diagnosis and the Decision

After the imaging, the scans, and a very honest conversation with my physio here, I had two choices: rush back the "normal" way and hope for the best, or actually rebuild my knee from the ground up so this never happens again. I chose the second path, and it changed everything about how I think of my body as an athlete.

Finding ATG

A teammate sent me a video of Ben Patrick (most people know him as the "Knees Over Toes Guy") and at first I was skeptical. Everything I'd been taught growing up was "don't let your knee go past your toes," and here was this guy building his entire training philosophy around doing exactly that, safely and progressively. But the more I watched, the more it made sense. Patrick built ATG (Athletic Truth Group) after going through his own years of knee pain and surgeries, and his whole approach is about strengthening the knee through its full range of motion instead of avoiding it.

So, I committed. Fully.

What My Recovery Actually Looked Like

I won't lie to you, this wasn't glamorous. It was repetitive, humbling, and slow in a way that tested every bit of patience I had. My days revolved around:

  • Backward sled walks to get blood flowing into the knee without aggravating it
  • Tibialis raises against a wall, which sound easy until you're doing them for the hundredth time
  • ATG split squats, progressing the depth and load week by week as my knee allowed it
  • Patellar tendon isometrics to manage pain and rebuild tolerance to load
  • Daily mobility work for my ankles and hips, because Patrick's whole philosophy is that the knee doesn't work in isolation, everything around it matters too

    • The discipline part is what nobody warns you about. It's easy to be motivated when you're scoring goals on the weekend. It's a completely different kind of discipline to show up every single day, sometimes twice a day, to do unglamorous rehab work in an empty corner of the gym while your teammates are out on the pitch.

    The Mental Side Nobody Talks About

    Studying computer science online while rehabbing actually helped more than I expected. It gave my brain somewhere else to go when the frustration got heavy. There's something almost meditative about debugging code that translated into how I approached debugging my own body… one small fix at a time, testing, adjusting, not getting emotional about setbacks.

    Where I Am Now

    Months later, I'm back on the pitch with local teams here in Madrid, and honestly, my knee feels more stable than it did before the injury. That's the part that still surprises me. I didn't just recover, I rebuilt something stronger.

    If you're a young player going through an injury right now, especially something knee-related, my biggest piece of advice is this: don't just wait for time to pass. Be active in your own recovery. Be disciplined about the boring stuff. The sled walks and the wall raises aren't exciting, but they're the reason I'm playing again.

    "Boricua hasta los huesos," and now, "hasta las rodillas too."
